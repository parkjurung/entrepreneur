# US Immigration And Ecosystem Access

Durable strategy notes for building US immigration and US ecosystem access as a long-term founder option.

This is not legal advice. Use it as a preparation and evidence-design memo before speaking with US immigration counsel.

## Core Principle

As of 2026-06-15, the US immigration track should remain downstream of successful company execution.

The goal is not to escape from Korea or from current operational pressure. The goal is to make the user's company-building record legible to the US ecosystem so that immigration, customers, investors, researchers, and deeptech networks become realistic options.

The strongest evidence base is:

- successful current company management,
- durable revenue and customer outcomes,
- AI tutor / realtime agent product proof,
- technical leadership in complex interactive AI systems,
- public and independent recognition,
- US-relevant relationships and demand signals.

Stronger positioning:

## Ethnicity, Citizenship, And US Network Access

As of 2026-06-15, the user's question is not only legal immigration strategy. It is also whether a Korea-born Korean founder can enter mainstream US elite networks and create meaningful impact without being confined to Korean American networks.

### Core View

The answer is: possible, but not by passive assimilation or generic networking.

The strongest path is to become strategically necessary to US frontier networks through a scarce contribution: a company, technology, dataset, customer access, infrastructure insight, policy relevance, or deeptech bridge that important US actors cannot easily ignore.

Korean identity should be treated as a root and source of differentiated assets, not as a ceiling. Korean American networks can be a base camp, but they are too small to be the final arena for frontier AI, deeptech, energy, or national-security-adjacent technology.

### Facts And External Signals

- US technology and AI entrepreneurship has been heavily shaped by immigrants. Public analyses reviewed on 2026-06-15 included CSET's analysis that 66% of the 2019 Forbes AI 50 companies had at least one immigrant founder, and NFAP's analysis that immigrants founded or cofounded more than half of US billion-dollar startups.
- Asian Americans are visibly represented in technical work but face a documented leadership gap. HBR / Ascend analyses and related research describe Asian Americans, especially East Asians, as less likely to move from individual contributor or professional roles into management and executive roles.
- Korean or Korea-born people have entered mainstream US institutions, but usually through unusually strong achievement, US institutional fluency, and durable sponsorship. Examples reviewed include:
  - Yejin Choi: Seoul National University background, Stanford HAI professor, MacArthur Fellow, and TIME100 AI recognition.
  - Jim Yong Kim: Korean-born physician and global health leader, Dartmouth president, and World Bank president.
  - James Park: Fitbit cofounder and CEO, later acquired by Google.
  - Tim Hwang: FiscalNote founder, with a company positioned in US policy, data, AI, and government-facing networks.

### Assumptions

- Frontier AI, deeptech, energy, and national-security-adjacent technology may increasingly depend on US-led trust networks, legal status, institutional affiliation, customers, capital, and policy alignment.
- A Korea-born founder entering as an adult will likely face more friction than someone raised inside US education and social institutions.
- East Asian / Korean founders may encounter stereotypes of being technically competent but not natural leaders. This makes narrative, executive presence, conflict fluency, and sponsorship more important.
- Founder status can partly route around corporate promotion barriers because customers, investors, and strategic partners respond to traction and necessity.

### Network Strategy

Build three layers in parallel:

1. Korean and Korean American networks: roots, trust, identity, Korea-US bridge, early introductions, and long-term cultural base.
2. Asian American, immigrant founder, and technical networks: high-density founder/operator access in AI and Silicon Valley-style ecosystems.
3. Mainstream US institutional networks: universities, research labs, top-tier investors, large customers, policy circles, national-interest technology networks, and deeptech operators.

Do not become dependent on Korean-only networks. Use them as leverage into broader networks.

### Practical Implications

- Build US anchors: US customers, US advisors, US investors, US public writing, US conference presence, US entity or project structure where there is real business purpose, and possibly US legal status.
- Develop non-Korean sponsors who can credibly introduce, defend, and explain the user's work inside US institutions.
- Make English-facing proof legible: technical essays, demos, metrics, customer outcomes, and a clear founder narrative.
- Position the user not as "a Korean founder asking to be included," but as "a Korean-rooted founder whose AI tutoring / realtime interactive agent expertise and Korea-US bridge create value for important US problems."

### Strategic Identity Sentence

> A Korea-formed founder entering the US AI/deeptech frontier, connecting Korean industrial and technical strengths with US frontier networks, without hiding Korean identity or being confined by it.

## Candidate Tracks

### O-1A

Purpose:

- Fast nonimmigrant work/status option for a founder with extraordinary ability.
- Useful if there is a US company, agent, customer, investor, accelerator, or project structure that supports the petition.

Evidence emphasis:

- leading or critical role,
- original business or technical contributions,
- major media or trade publication coverage,
- judging others' work,
- high compensation or commercial success,
- expert letters backed by documentary evidence.

Operational note:

- O-1A can be a practical first filing target if the evidence package becomes strong enough before EB-1A.

### EB-1A

Purpose:

- Immigrant petition for extraordinary ability.
- Stronger and harder version of the O-1A-style narrative.

Evidence emphasis:

- sustained national or international acclaim,
- proof that the user is among the small percentage at the top of the field,
- intent to continue work in the same area of expertise,
- substantial prospective benefit to the United States.

Operational note:

- Do not rush unless independent recognition and contribution evidence are strong. O-1A approval can help but does not guarantee EB-1A.

### EB-2 NIW

Purpose:

- Immigrant petition where the job offer and labor certification requirement can be waived if the proposed endeavor is in the US national interest.

Evidence emphasis:

- a specific proposed endeavor with substantial merit and national importance,
- proof the user is well positioned to advance it,
- evidence that waiving the job offer / labor certification requirement benefits the United States on balance.

Operational note:

- Recent data suggests NIW scrutiny has tightened. The petition should not be framed as "I am a good founder" but as "this specific US-relevant endeavor matters, and I am unusually well positioned to advance it."

### E-2

Purpose:

- Tactical nonimmigrant option for a Korean national investing in and directing a US business.

Evidence emphasis:

- Korean nationality,
- substantial investment,
- real and operating enterprise,
- active direction and development of the business,
- credible business plan and operating evidence.

Operational note:

- Useful for US presence if a real US business emerges. It is not itself a green card path.

### International Entrepreneur Rule

Purpose:

- Possible parole route for a US startup with substantial potential for rapid growth and job creation.

Evidence emphasis:

- US startup,
- qualifying investment or government awards/grants,
- ownership and active role,
- growth and job-creation potential.

Operational note:

- Keep as a secondary option if the company creates a US startup with US investor or grant support.

- These percentages are category-wide environment signals, not personal odds. They mix weak and strong petitions, different attorneys, different evidence quality, and cases received in earlier periods.

## Evidence Portfolio To Build While Running The Company

### 1. Leading Or Critical Role

Goal:

- Show the user was not merely a CEO by title but a critical technical and business leader.

Evidence to preserve:

- contracts and scopes of work,
- customer-facing project documents,
- org charts and responsibility maps,
- product roadmaps and decision records,
- major architecture decisions,
- GitHub / PR / commit evidence where safe to disclose,
- internal memos showing the user's strategic and technical leadership,
- customer or team letters describing the user's specific role.

### 2. Original Contributions Of Major Significance

Goal:

- Convert AI tutor and realtime canvas work into evidence of original technical/business contribution.

Evidence to preserve or create:

- technical architecture memo for multimodal realtime AI tutoring,
- before/after performance or quality metrics,
- evaluation harness results,
- incident retrospectives showing hard problems solved,
- customer adoption and continued investment,
- patents or provisional patent analysis where appropriate,
- sanitized demos or videos,
- expert letters tied to concrete artifacts,
- open-source components or public technical resources if strategically safe.

### 3. Published Material About The User Or Work

Goal:

- Obtain independent recognition outside the user's own company and immediate network.

Preferred evidence:

- articles in AI, edtech, startup, or technical trade publications,
- podcasts or interviews where the user and work are discussed in substance,
- conference profiles or case studies,
- third-party reports mentioning Pagecall / AI tutor work and the user's role.

Weak evidence:

- paid PR,
- company blog posts alone,
- passing mentions without discussion of the user's work.

### 4. Judging The Work Of Others

Goal:

- Build a clean, low-distraction evidence category.

Possible activities:

- judge AI/edtech hackathons,
- review accelerator or startup competition applications,
- mentor or judge at AI education programs,
- review conference submissions or technical demos,
- participate in grant or investment screening panels if invited.

Evidence to preserve:

- invitation,
- criteria,
- proof of completed judging,
- list of judges if public,
- event reputation and reach.

### 5. Scholarly Or Professional Authorship

Goal:

- Build technical credibility beyond sales materials.

Target artifacts:

- 3-5 English technical essays or whitepapers,
- workshop submissions,
- conference talks,
- case studies on AI tutoring architecture, realtime multimodal tutoring, canvas-grounded evaluation, and learning-agent safety.

Possible titles:

- "Realtime Multimodal AI Tutoring Over Canvas Interfaces"
- "Evaluation Loops For Canvas-Grounded Math Tutoring Agents"
- "Human-In-The-Loop Safety Patterns For AI Learning Interfaces"

### 6. Independent Expert Letters

Goal:

- Use letters as corroboration, not as the foundation.

Target writers:

- customer executives with direct knowledge,
- US edtech founders or operators,
- AI infrastructure or realtime systems experts,
- education researchers,
- investors or accelerator leaders,
- domain experts who can explain field significance.

Letter quality bar:

- specific factual basis,
- direct knowledge of the user's work,
- explanation of field significance,
- connection to objective documents,
- writer's own qualifications.

### 7. US Nexus

Goal:

- Show the endeavor is connected to US demand and US national interest.

Evidence to build:

- US customer discovery calls,
- US school / tutoring / edtech LOIs,
- pilot proposals,
- US advisor relationships,
- US investor conversations,
- US conference participation,
- market memo tying AI tutoring to US math/STEM learning problems,
- possible US entity or agent structure if there is real business purpose.

## Milestones

- Speak with at least two US startup / AI immigration lawyers.
- Build a case matrix for O-1A, EB-1A, EB-2 NIW, E-2, and International Entrepreneur Rule.
- Categorize current evidence as strong, medium, weak, or missing.
- Identify which evidence can be disclosed publicly and which must remain confidential due to customer/IP constraints.

### 1-3 Months

- Create an English evidence dossier for Pagecall and AI tutor work.
- Write the first public-safe technical narrative.
- Gather contracts, revenue proof, customer outcomes, technical architecture summaries, and role evidence.
- Start US network interviews with founders, researchers, customers, investors, and immigration professionals.

### 3-6 Months

- Build O-1A-ready evidence around at least three categories:
  - critical role,
  - original contribution,
  - published material,
  - judging,
  - high compensation / commercial success if applicable.
- Design a US agent, employer, customer, or project structure with counsel if O-1A is plausible.
- Obtain initial US LOIs, pilot conversations, or advisor support if possible.

### 6-12 Months

- Decide whether O-1A filing is realistic.
- Produce 2-3 additional English technical or professional artifacts.
- Secure at least 1-2 credible US demand signals.
- Draft an NIW proposed endeavor around AI-native tutoring infrastructure and US education/STEM impact.

### 12-18 Months

- Decide whether to file EB-2 NIW.
- Strengthen national-importance evidence through US customer/researcher/advisor letters and public artifacts.
- Continue to compound company evidence: revenue durability, customer renewal, product adoption, measurable outcomes.

### 18-36 Months

- Re-evaluate EB-1A.
- File EB-1A only if independent recognition, contribution significance, public evidence, and expert support are much stronger.
- If a real US business has emerged, evaluate E-2 or a US startup structure as a tactical presence option.

## Operating Rules

- Do not let immigration preparation reduce the odds of company success.
- Every immigration evidence action should ideally also improve the business: better customer proof, better technical narrative, better sales collateral, better investor readiness, better recruiting story.
- Prefer independent, public, verifiable evidence over private praise.
- Preserve documents as they are created; reconstructing evidence later is costly and weaker.
- Keep facts, assumptions, recommendations, and legal conclusions separate.
- Treat attorney advice as required before filing or making material immigration decisions.
