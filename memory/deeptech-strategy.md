# Deeptech Strategy

Durable notes on the user's long-term desire to enter deeptech, especially energy, without losing focus on current company execution.

## Core Motivation

As of 2026-06-15:

- The user wants to eventually work on larger civilizational problems such as energy, AI, quantum technology, and the future of humanity.
- Energy currently feels more emotionally attractive than quantum because the user studied chemistry, likes physics, and is drawn to physical infrastructure.
- The user is concerned about spending a lifetime only making games despite having larger ambitions.
- The current company is already executing AI tutor work and Alcanthia, so deeptech should be treated as a long-term entry strategy rather than an immediate pivot.

## Strategic Conclusion

The realistic path is not to start a capital-intensive energy company immediately.

The recommended path is:

1. Use the current company to build capital, founder credibility, and a stronger exit option.
2. Preserve and sharpen the user's identity as a founder who can productize complex AI, realtime, interactive, and simulation-heavy systems.
3. Build deeptech network access slowly, especially with PhD-level technical founders in energy, chemistry, materials, plasma, and control systems.
4. Enter deeptech later as a commercializing CEO / cofounder paired with a strong technical inventor, not as a solo inventor trying to compete with labs or hyperscalers.

## Software In The Agent Era

The user believes software implementation itself is becoming close to free because coding agents will increasingly make ordinary software easy to produce.

Working strategic belief:

- Do not build a company whose main asset is "we can write software for this problem."
- Software remains valuable when it packages or compounds a scarce non-software asset.
- Durable software businesses should be tied to at least one of:
  - proprietary data or repeated learning loops,
  - workflow ownership,
  - measurable outcomes and accountability,
  - trust in high-stakes domains,
  - real-world control rights,
  - integration into difficult physical or organizational systems,
  - taste, worldbuilding, fun, or aesthetic judgment.

Weak software plays:

- Generic SaaS features.
- "AI dashboard" or "AI automation" wrappers without proprietary data, control rights, or workflow ownership.
- External software sold to companies that already have strong internal teams, data, and operating authority.
- Products whose natural customer response is "why don't you join our company and lead this internally?"

Stronger software-mediated plays:

- Products born from the company's own repeated operational pain.
- Tools where Pagecall can be its own first customer before selling externally.
- Systems that compound through real usage data, domain trust, or operational responsibility.
- Games and interactive products where the product's core scarcity is taste, fun, worldbuilding, and live-operation judgment rather than code alone.

## Why Games Still Make Strategic Sense

The user originally considered games because games require more than code:

- creativity,
- aesthetic judgment,
- feel for fun,
- worldbuilding,
- progression and economy design,
- live operation,
- retention and payment psychology.

In the agent era, this may be more defensible than generic B2B software because the scarce asset is not implementation speed alone.

Alcanthia should therefore be interpreted not only as a game project, but also as a way to test whether the user and team can build a product whose core value is taste, retention, simulation, and operation.

## Deeptech Entry Candidates

### AI Infra x Energy Control Software

Initial attraction:

- AI data centers create major energy, cooling, and grid-pressure problems.
- The area connects AI, energy, infrastructure, optimization, and control systems.

Critical objection:

- Hyperscalers such as Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, and NVIDIA have internal infra, energy, operations, and software teams.
- If the product is generic optimization software, they can likely build it internally using their own data and agents.
- Pagecall does not currently operate data centers, GPU fleets, power contracts, cooling systems, or grid assets.
- A direct pitch risks becoming a hiring conversation rather than a startup opportunity.

Revised view:

- Do not pursue this as generic software for hyperscalers.
- Only revisit if Pagecall can get real operational pain, data, customer access, or control rights.
- More plausible future wedges could involve non-hyperscaler GPU clouds, colocation operators, private AI clusters, demand response, workload-aware energy orchestration, or software layers between GPU workloads and physical energy constraints.

### Fusion / Tokamak Control Software

Initial attraction:

- Strong fit with the user's interest in physics, energy, control, and science-fiction-scale problems.

Critical objection:

- Real fusion control requires plasma physics expertise, experimental access, safety validation, institutional trust, and device data.
- Software alone is not enough.

Revised view:

- Treat fusion control as a long-term option, not a near-term Pagecall business.
- Pursue only with a strong PhD-level domain cofounder and access to a lab, fusion startup, or experimental data.
- The user's possible role is AI/control/product/commercialization founder, not plasma-science inventor.

### Tokamak Components / Supply Chain

Initial attraction:

- A more tangible deeptech energy path.

Critical objection:

- Manufacturing, certification, long procurement cycles, and capital requirements make this a poor near-term founder-market fit.

Revised view:

- Avoid direct components manufacturing for now.
- If relevant later, prefer inspection, simulation, QA, digital twin, or operational software around advanced components rather than owning the manufacturing stack.

### Chemistry / Materials / Energy Research Automation

Current best-fit deeptech wedge candidate:

- Autonomous experimentation and AI-agent workflows for chemistry, materials, batteries, catalysts, energy storage, or advanced materials labs.

Why it fits:

- The user studied chemistry and computer science.
- It connects natural science, AI agents, software, experimentation, data, and potential energy impact.
- It does not require Pagecall to own massive physical infrastructure at the start.
- It can pair naturally with a PhD technical founder who has lab access or a concrete invention.
- It can become a bridge toward energy, materials, batteries, fusion materials, or scientific instrumentation.

Main caveat:

- It still needs real lab workflow access and domain credibility. It should not be built from imagination alone.

## Realistic Five-Year Scenario

1. 2026-2027: Keep Pagecall alive and cash-flow positive through AI tutor work, Pagecall legacy revenue, and Alcanthia validation.
2. 2026-2027: Spend limited weekly time building deeptech literacy and network access, without distracting from current execution.
3. 2027: Speak with 30-50 researchers, founders, operators, and investors across energy, materials, chemistry automation, fusion, scientific instrumentation, and control systems.
4. 2027-2028: Identify one or two PhD-level potential cofounders and jointly produce technical memos, market maps, or small prototypes.
5. 2028-2029: If Pagecall has an exit or strong enough cash flow, create or join a deeptech company as a commercializing CEO/cofounder with a meaningful ownership position.

Meaningful ownership means founder-level ownership. Advisor or small angel ownership does not satisfy the user's goal of taking a meaningful part in a large deeptech outcome.

## Practical Operating Rule

Deeptech exploration should be maintained as a deliberate long-term option, not allowed to dilute current execution.

Recommended cadence:

- Allocate a small fixed weekly time budget for deeptech study and network building.
- Focus on learning from real operators, researchers, and founders rather than abstract idea generation.
- Evaluate ideas by whether Pagecall can obtain data, control rights, trust, workflow access, or a strong technical cofounder.
- Prefer problems where the user's current founder experience creates cofounder-level leverage rather than employee-level leverage.
